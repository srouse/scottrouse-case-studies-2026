# Research Synthesis: Healthcare AI Lead Designer Portfolio Exemplars

**Research ID**: 001-healthcare-ai-lead-designer-portfolio-exemplars  
**Completed**: 2026-04-14  
**Status**: Final report (Phase 1 complete; per-exemplar rubrics not finished—see Limitations)

---

## Executive Summary

This research asked which **observable portfolio patterns**—information architecture, case-study depth, AI-specific narrative, healthcare-adjacent trust signals, and visual evidence—recur in **strong public portfolios** aligned with a **Lead Designer, AI products** profile in **healthcare / oncology**, and how those patterns compare to **credible design and health-UX guidance**.

Execution to date combined **Phase 1 broad exploration**: ten distinct expert or editorial sources (NN/g, healthcare UX practitioner writing, AI-portfolio roundups, and 2025–2026 UX guidance) plus a **catalog of twelve public portfolio URLs** tagged for domain fit (**H+AI**, **Health**, **AI-prod**, **Product**). The evidence converges on a **repeatable case-study skeleton** (problem, role, methods, decisions and tradeoffs, outcomes, reflection), **scannable first-screen clarity**, and—where AI is claimed—**concrete product and evaluation evidence** rather than labels alone (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.; Rezende, 2025; Bestfolios, 2026; Lovable, 2026).

**Healthcare-specific** public material stresses **process storytelling**, **role clarity**, and **domain-appropriate humility** (McCurdy, n.d.). **Oncology-specific** public portfolios with both **health** and **lead-level AI product** narrative were **not identified** in this pass; the seed list instead combines **health-adjacent** and **AI-product** exemplars—a **gap** that limits direct answers to “oncology + AI lead” and should be closed with deeper search or **redacted private case studies** if public examples remain sparse.

**Overall conclusion:** For this role, the strongest *documented* pattern is **layered presentation**: a short, credible value proposition for a 30-second scan, plus **deep case studies** that show **scope, stakeholders, tradeoffs, and systems**—with explicit **AI behavior, failure modes, and evaluation** where ML is in scope. Confidence in **pattern-level** conclusions is **medium-high** (multiple independent guidance sources agree). Confidence in **which named exemplars best instantiate oncology + AI** is **low** until rubric reviews and/or better domain matches are completed.

## Research Question Recap

**Primary:** What observable portfolio patterns appear most often among strong public portfolios relevant to a **Lead Designer, AI products** role in **healthcare / oncology**, and how do those patterns compare to **expert guidance** from credible design and health-UX sources?

**Secondary:** Which **evaluation questions** best separate a generic product portfolio from a **lead-level, AI + healthcare-ready** portfolio?

**Context:** The audience is fluent in AI product design, healthcare or high-stakes domains, and design craft (e.g., hiring committee, mentor, or personal quality bar). The constitution treats exemplars as **informed practice**, not proof of hiring outcomes (`.researchkit/memory/constitution.md`).

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: A convergent case-study structure appears across authoritative UX guidance and practitioner writing

**Summary statement:** Strong portfolios repeatedly organize work as **problem → role and collaboration → methods → decisions/tradeoffs → outcomes → learnings**, with **process visuals** rather than finals alone (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.), and as a **narrative argument** about why choices mattered (Pan, 2024, as summarized in Case Study Club; McCurdy, n.d.).

**Evidence base** (3+ sources):

1. **Nielsen Norman Group (n.d.)**: Recommends **3–5 projects** as detailed case studies aligned with the job; case studies should include problem/hypothesis, role, methods, results/impact, and process visuals; content should be **scannable** (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.).
2. **Pan (2024)** (interview, Case Study Club): Stresses **distilling projects into narrative** and connecting the story to **why decisions mattered**—consistent with “structured argument” framing (Pan, 2024).
3. **McCurdy (n.d.)**: For healthcare-oriented designers, emphasizes **process storytelling**, showing **how you work**, and clarifying **your role** on team projects (McCurdy, n.d.).

**Confidence level:** **High** for “what these sources recommend.”  
**Reasoning:** NN/g is rated **credible secondary** in project terms; McCurdy and the interview add **triangulation** on narrative and role. **Lower confidence** for automatic transfer to **oncology AI lead** without domain-specific exemplars.

**Implications:** Candidates targeting **lead** roles should expect reviewers to look for **accountability**—not only screens—for **process, collaboration, and outcomes** (McCurdy, n.d.; Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.).

---

### Finding 2: Attention economics favor a clear “above the fold” identity—tension with depth is managed by layering

**Summary statement:** Reviewers often spend **seconds to a few minutes** on first pass; the portfolio must quickly communicate **who you are**, **what you excel at**, and **where the work lives** (Rezende, 2025). Editorial sources also stress **side-by-side comparison** by hiring teams, favoring **clarity and reasoning under constraints** (UXfolio, n.d.). This **does not** negate long case studies; it implies a **summary layer** plus detail (Rezende, 2025; Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.).

**Evidence base:**

1. **Rezende (2025):** Many reviews are **30 seconds–4 minutes**; first scan must convey identity and strengths; strong case studies read as **structured arguments** with paths explored and rejected (Rezende, 2025).
2. **UXfolio (n.d.):** Hiring **compares candidates**; clarity, reasoning, and explanation under constraints matter; familiar structure helps reviewers (UXfolio, n.d.).
3. **Nielsen Norman Group (n.d.):** **Scannable** case studies and **web-based** portfolios as common format—aligns with designing for quick navigation into depth (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.).

**Confidence level:** **Medium-high** for the **attention-window** claim (stated in Rezende, 2025—treat timing as **heuristic**, not measured universal).  
**Reasoning:** Multiple sources agree on **scannability + depth**; exact times vary by context.

**Implications:** For **healthcare AI**, a concise **trust and responsibility** line (privacy, safety, clinician workflow) may belong in the **first screen** or project summaries—paired with deeper case content (synthesis from McCurdy, n.d., and plan rubric).

---

### Finding 3: “AI credibility” in portfolios is shown through concrete UX, workflows, metrics, and evaluation—not the word “AI” alone

**Summary statement:** Curated discussions of **AI in portfolios** highlight **embedded assistants**, **interactive experiments**, and **deep AI product case studies** (Bestfolios, 2026). Guides describing **RAG** or **chatbot** portfolios warn that success depends on the **assistant’s accuracy** and **underlying case content** (Lovable, 2026). This matches the review dimension **AI credibility** in the plan: concrete UI, workflow, metrics, or evaluation.

**Evidence base:**

1. **Bestfolios (2026):** Lists patterns such as embedded assistant, experiments, and deep case studies—not keyword-only use (Bestfolios, 2026).
2. **Lovable (2026):** Describes **chatbot/RAG** portfolio mechanics and ties success to **accurate assistant** and solid **case content** (Lovable, 2026)—flags **vendor bias** but documents **mechanisms** (also noted in `findings.md`).
3. **Rezende (2025):** Frames differentiation through **craft** and **critical thinking** in a crowded market—consistent with evidence-backed AI narratives (Rezende, 2025).

**Confidence level:** **Medium** for **pattern** claims (editorial and vendor-tied guides; possible **sponsorship** bias per findings log).  
**Reasoning:** Triangulation across **independent editorial** (Bestfolios, 2026; Rezende, 2025) and **how-to** guide (Lovable, 2026) increases confidence in **what to show**, not in **which tool** to use.

**Implications:** For **healthcare**, reviewers may penalize **opaque “AI magic”**; portfolios should surface **failure modes**, **guardrails**, and **evaluation** where possible (professional judgment informed by Lovable, 2026; McCurdy, n.d.).

---

### Finding 4: Healthcare-ready signals emphasize process, ethics-adjacent framing, and trust—public “H+AI” exemplars are few in the current seed

**Summary statement:** **McCurdy (n.d.)** frames healthcare design portfolios around **expected** portfolios, **process** over polish alone, and **role** clarity. The **exemplar seed list** includes **Health** (e.g., Katie McCurdy) and **H+AI** (e.g., Pearl Sequeira) but **does not** yet include **oncology-specific** URLs; direct **H+AI** count is **limited** (`sources.md` coverage check).

**Evidence base:**

1. **McCurdy (n.d.)**: Healthcare hiring **expects** portfolios; weight on **process** and **how you work** (McCurdy, n.d.).
2. **Seed catalog** (`sources.md`): Tags include **Health** and **H+AI**; explicit note that **oncology** portfolios were **not yet identified**.
3. **Plan rubric** (embedded in research design): Calls for **trust** signals—anonymization, safety, clinician workflow, humility (plan.md)—used as **evaluation lens** for future rubric completion.

**Confidence level:** **Medium** for “healthcare portfolios should emphasize process and trust”—**Low** for “these twelve are the best oncology-AI lead examples.”  
**Reasoning:** Healthcare guidance is **single-author Medium** (informed practice); exemplar table is **not rubric-validated**.

**Implications:** Until **more H+AI** or **oncology-adjacent** public cases are found, synthesis for **oncology + AI lead** should lean on **pattern combination** (health process + AI evidence) and **explicit limitation** (McCurdy, n.d.; `sources.md`).

---

### Finding 5: Lead-level signals (scope, stakeholders, systems, metrics) are called out in guidance and seed notes—but need per-site verification

**Summary statement:** `findings.md` notes **leadership** patterns such as **org scale, design system, metrics** (referencing Diego VZ in emerging themes). **NN/g**-style case study guidance supports **role and collaboration** and outcomes (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.). **Webflow (2025)** and **Designlab (2026)** roundups often feature **visually strong** sites—**style vs. substance** risk for healthcare AI readers (Webflow, 2025; Designlab, 2026).

**Evidence base:**

1. **Nielsen Norman Group (n.d.)**: Role, collaboration, methods, impact (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.).
2. **Designlab (2026)** / **Webflow (2025)**: Illustrate **student/clarity** and **award-style** portfolios—useful as **contrasts** for what “lead” must add (Designlab, 2026; Webflow, 2025).
3. **Internal research notes** (`findings.md`): Diego VZ cited for **scope** signals—**anecdotal** until rubric applied.

**Confidence level:** **Medium** for “lead portfolios should show scope beyond screens”; **Low** for any **named** exemplar until rubric-complete.  
**Reasoning:** Constitution: **design conventions** benefit from **2+ sources or 1 authoritative + exemplars as illustrations not proof**—here exemplars are **not yet systematically scored**.

**Implications:** **Evaluation questions** for hiring (plan secondary question) should explicitly probe **stakeholder complexity**, **tradeoffs**, **systems**, and **AI evaluation**—not only visual craft.

## Analysis & Discussion

### Cross-cutting themes

- **Convergence:** Problem–process–outcome **story architecture** appears in **NN/g**, **case-study practitioner discourse**, and **2025–2026** UX articles (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.; Pan, 2024; Rezende, 2025).
- **Tension:** **Minimal** portfolio aesthetics vs. **maximal** case depth—resolved in guidance by **scannable structure** and **layering** (Rezende, 2025; Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.; UXfolio, n.d.).
- **AI + health:** Public synthesis currently **combines** health **framing** (McCurdy, n.d.) with **AI evidence** patterns (Bestfolios, 2026; Lovable, 2026) because **sparse** combined public exemplars were documented (`sources.md`).

### Conflicting viewpoints

| Issue | Position A | Position B | Assessment |
|-------|------------|------------|------------|
| **Visual polish vs. process** | Roundups celebrate **award-style** presentation (Webflow, 2025) | Healthcare guidance stresses **process and role** over polish alone (McCurdy, n.d.) | For **healthcare AI lead**, **substance + clarity** should dominate; visuals support **evidence** (diagrams, workflows), not decoration (synthesis judgment; McCurdy, n.d.; Webflow, 2025). |
| **AI chatbot/RAG as portfolio shell** | Guides describe **novel** interaction patterns (Lovable, 2026; Ixca, 2025) | Accuracy and content risk **hurting trust** if wrong (Lovable, 2026) | Use **only** if **reliability** is high; healthcare reviewers may be **less forgiving** of wrong answers (Lovable, 2026; professional judgment). |

### Limitations

1. **Incomplete execution vs. plan:** **Per-exemplar rubrics** for all **≥12** sites are **not** finished (`findings.md` checklist)—patterns about **specific** sites remain **preliminary**.
2. **Survivorship and curation bias:** “Best portfolio” lists overweight **visibility** and **English-language** designers (`findings.md`; `sources.md` bias log).
3. **Oncology gap:** No **oncology-specific** portfolio documented—conclusions for **oncology + AI lead** are **inferential**.
4. **Self-reported outcomes:** Portfolio **impact** claims are **not independently verified** (constitution).
5. **Source types:** **No peer-reviewed** studies in the bibliography yet—limits **evidence strength** for hiring **causality**.

## Conclusions

### Direct answer to the research question

Among **guidance sources** and **Phase 1 exemplar seeds**, the most **frequent observable patterns** relevant to a **Lead Designer, AI products** profile in **healthcare / oncology** are: **(1)** clear **IA** and **first-screen** positioning; **(2)** **case studies** with explicit **role, methods, tradeoffs, outcomes** (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.; Pan, 2024; McCurdy, n.d.); **(3)** **AI narratives** backed by **concrete** UX, workflows, and evaluation concepts (Bestfolios, 2026; Lovable, 2026; Rezende, 2025); **(4)** **healthcare-adjacent** emphasis on **process**, **collaboration**, and **trust-appropriate** framing (McCurdy, n.d.). **Comparison to expert guidance** shows **alignment** on structure and scannability; **exemplar-level** confirmation for **oncology + AI** is **not yet established** in this dataset (`sources.md`).

### Key takeaways

1. **Use a layered IA:** Home → work → about/contact with **scannable** case summaries and **deep** case pages (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.; Rezende, 2025).
2. **Treat AI as a design problem:** Show **flows, failure considerations, and evaluation**—not only branding (Bestfolios, 2026; Lovable, 2026).
3. **Healthcare lead signal:** Emphasize **how you work with complexity**, **role clarity**, and **ethical/trust** awareness in narrative (McCurdy, n.d.).
4. **Name the gap:** Actively seek or **construct** **oncology-relevant** evidence (public or **redacted**) because the current seed list **does not** prove oncology-specific patterns (`sources.md`).
5. **Use the plan’s rubric** as the **discriminating question set** for “generic” vs “lead AI + healthcare-ready” until empirical hiring data is added (plan.md).

### Confidence assessment

**Overall:** **Medium** for **pattern recommendations**; **Low** for **oncology-specific exemplar** claims.

**Reasoning:**

- **Source quality:** Mix of **credible secondary** (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.) and **informed practice** (McCurdy, n.d.; editorials)—appropriate for **portfolio norms**, not for **regulatory** claims.
- **Consistency:** Multiple sources agree on **case structure** and **attention limits** (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.; Rezende, 2025; UXfolio, n.d.).
- **Coverage:** **Exemplar analysis incomplete**; **oncology** missing.
- **Recency:** Strong for **2025–2026** AI-portfolio discourse (Bestfolios, 2026; Lovable, 2026; Rezende, 2025).

## Recommendations

### For practice (portfolio build)

1. **Draft each case** on the shared skeleton: problem; constraints; **your role**; methods; **AI specifics** (if any): model-in-loop, failure modes, evaluation; outcomes; reflection (synthesis of Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.; Pan, 2024; McCurdy, n.d.).
2. **Add a “healthcare AI lead”** one-screen manifesto: scope, stakeholders, systems, metrics—mirroring **lead** dimensions from the plan rubric (plan.md).
3. **If using RAG/chat UX** for the portfolio itself, invest in **accuracy** and **fallbacks**—healthcare audiences may infer product judgment from the demo (Lovable, 2026).
4. **Revisit URLs** on a schedule—portfolio **churn** is a known limitation (`findings.md`).

### For further research

1. Complete **Phase 2**: Full **rubric** for all **12** exemplars; add replacements if links die (`sources.md`).
2. **Targeted search** for **oncology UX**, **clinical decision support design**, and **health AI** portfolios; document **scarcity** with dates if still sparse.
3. Add **1–2 authoritative** sources on **human factors / CDS** *presentation* if claims touch **clinical workflow** (FDA-style HF docs)—only when relevant (constitution).
4. **Optional:** Small **n** hiring-manager interview to validate **evaluation questions** (plan secondary question)—not present in current evidence.

### Update schedule

- **Review** guidance and exemplar list **every 6–12 months** or when **AI portfolio norms** shift (tooling and evaluator expectations change quickly—Rezende, 2025; Bestfolios, 2026).
